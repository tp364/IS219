Why this project matters

The original app already worked as a tool, but this page explains why the redesign begins with the human problem. A visitor has to feel the question before they care about the chart.

The project starts with a transition problem

Recent graduates leave school into a market shaped by salaries, rent, debt, and interest rates. Housing is one of the clearest places where those pressures become visible. The project asks a focused version of that reality: could a new graduate reach a median-priced home within five years?

That question turns a vague fear into a testable claim. It also gives the project a clean narrative line: pressure, question, method, evidence, then tool.

The scrollytelling homepage uses this page as a slower branch: the story introduces the stakes, and this page expands them.

Why the new homepage begins with story

Emotion first

The visitor needs a reason to keep scrolling before they see a calculator or a dataset.

Claim second

The redesign narrows the topic into a concrete five-year affordability test instead of a broad opinion.

Evidence third

Once the story earns the visitor's attention, the chart and tool feel like proof instead of decoration.

A better narrative sequence made the data easier to trust and the interface easier to approach.

That is the central difference between the earlier prototype and the scrollytelling version. The tool is still there, but the page now teaches before it asks the visitor to interact.
